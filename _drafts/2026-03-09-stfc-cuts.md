Since around the start of February, there has been significant discourse about the future of UK research funding. 
Much of the conversation has been around the transition to Prof. Sir Patrick Vallance's buckets, an approach to simplify the research funding landscape and more easily relate funding to real world impacts that can be communicated to the public. 
The other area of discussion has been related to the [substantial budget reductions for STFC (Science, Technology and Facilties Council)](https://www.researchprofessionalnews.com/rr-news-uk-research-councils-2026-1-uk-physics-research-and-science-facilities-face-substantial-cuts/). 
This has included open letters from [Early Career Researchers in the Particle Physics, Astronomy and Nuclear (PPAN) community](https://ecr-openletter-stfc.github.io) and the [Heads of Physics](https://www.iop.org/about/news/open-letter-science-minister-heads-physics) across the UK, which have focused on concerns that the decrease in STFC budget will have on applicant-led research that is funded by STFC. 
In this blog, I would like to give a slightly different perspective, that of a researcher working in the chemical sciences, whose research necessitates the use of [UK's National Laboratories](https://www.ukri.org/who-we-are/stfc/facilities/) that are funded by STFC. 



Since around the start of February, there has been significant discourse about the future of UK research funding. 
In particular, the transition to Sir Patrick Vallance's buckets and the substantial budget reductions for STFC (Science, Technology and Facilities Council) by the end of 2029/2030. 
I would catagorise this discourse in two camps: discussion of the buckets (and associated concerns regarding the future of "Applicant-led research") and worries about the future funding of curiosity-driven research in particle physics and astronomy (i.e., see the [letter from the Heads of Phsyics](https://www.iop.org/about/news/open-letter-science-minister-heads-physics)), areas of research that are supported by STFC. 
In this blog, I want to give a slightly different perspective, that of a researcher working in the chemical sciences that makes significant use of the [UK's National Laboratories](https://www.ukri.org/who-we-are/stfc/facilities/). 

# Minister Vallance's Buckets 

UKRI's new approach for supporting research aims to simplify their [previously complex strategy into three buckets](https://www.ukri.org/wp-content/uploads/2026/03/260302-Sankey-diagram-for-publishing-v1-copy.pdf): 
- Curiosity-driven research
- Strategic government and societal proirities

Success in science takes three things, brilliant ideas, clever hardworking people, and access to first-class facilites. 
Here, I use the word facilities in a general term; it could be anything from the right stirrer plate to mix biological solutions to the bleeding edge in nuclear magnetic resonance instrumentation. 
Without all three parts of this, research can fail. 

I believe that the buckets language that has been used by UKRI risks overlooking the importance of such facilities.
