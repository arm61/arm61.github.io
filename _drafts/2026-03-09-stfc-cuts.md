Since around the start of February, there has been significant discourse about the future of UK research funding. 
Much of the conversation has been around the transition to Prof. Sir Patrick Vallance's buckets, an approach to simplify the research funding landscape and more easily relate funding to real-world impacts that can be communicated to the public. 
The other area of discussion has been related to the [substantial budget reductions for STFC (Science, Technology and Facilities Council)](https://www.researchprofessionalnews.com/rr-news-uk-research-councils-2026-1-uk-physics-research-and-science-facilities-face-substantial-cuts/). 
The feedback from some communities has been clear, with open letters from the [Early Career Researchers in the Particle Physics, Astronomy and Nuclear (PPAN) community](https://ecr-openletter-stfc.github.io) and the [Heads of Physics](https://www.iop.org/about/news/open-letter-science-minister-heads-physics) across the UK. 
These letters have focused on concerns about how a decrease in the STFC budget will affect applicant-led research funded by STFC. 
In this blog, I would like to offer a slightly different perspective: that of a researcher working in the chemical sciences, whose research requires the use of [UK's National Laboratories](https://www.ukri.org/who-we-are/stfc/facilities/) funded by STFC, and who is concerned that my community is not making our voices heard.

# What STFC Funds

UKRI (UK Research and Innovation) is split into a series of funding councils, which have acted relatively autonomously to support research. 
For example, EPSRC, which focuses on Engineering and the Physical Science[^1], funds scientific research often through what is referred to as "applicant-led research", where a scientist or group of scientists proposes some cutting-edge research they will conduct and EPSRC funds salaries of staff and other expenses (e.g., the purchase of chemicals or other technical requirements). 
EPSRC employs some staff, though they are principally focused on administering funding for EPSRC projects; i.e., they are (for the most part) not conducting research themselves. 

It has been acknowledged that STFC is a bit of a different beast from the other councils[^2]. 
STFC, alongside funding applicant-led research in PPAN, operates several laboratories and large-scale research facilities across the UK, including [ISIS Neutron and Muon Source](https://www.isis.stfc.ac.uk/) and [Diamond Light Source](https://www.diamond.ac.uk/Home.html), and is responsible for the UK's investment in international research collaborations such as the [European Space Agency](https://www.esa.int), [CERN](https://home.cern), and the [European Spallation Source](https://europeanspallationsource.se). 
Operating large-scale facilities and engaging in international collaborations pose very different challenges to the funding of applicant-led research, particularly given the long timescales of the funding required and the difficulty of responding to external economic changes[^3]. 
It is these challenges that UKRI identified as the cause of the "unique situation at STFC"[^2]. 

# How STFC Cuts Will Influence Other Councils

As highlighted above, there has been substantial pushback against the cuts to STFC from researchers who receive applicant-led research funding from STFC (i.e., those in PPAN). 
But I have not seen the same discussion of the influence that cuts to the STFC budgets will have on research supported by the other funding councils. 
I am not eligible to apply for STFC applicant-led funding; instead, I would be supported by EPSRC, which funds research in the chemical sciences. 
Despite this, I rely on the use of STFC-operated or supported research facilities to carry out my science; I am a regular visitor to [ISIS Neutron and Muon Source](https://www.isis.stfc.ac.uk/), the [Institut Laue-Langevin](https://ill.eu), and [Diamond Light Source](https://www.diamond.ac.uk/Home.html) and depend on these facilities to achieve this work. 
Furthermore, I am not alone; the figure below shows that the vast majority of UKRI-funded research conducted at Diamond Light Source is not funded by STFC.
Instead, EPSRC, BBSRC, and MRC make up over 90% of UKRI-funded research.

If we look at the list of [STFC Facilities](https://www.ukri.org/who-we-are/stfc/facilities/)[^5], Diamond is not the only STFC facility that drives non-"STFC science"[^5]. 
Cuts to facilities resulting from a budget reduction for STFC will affect all areas of UK science.
And currently, I feel that the users of these facilities are not making enough noise about this. 
If we do not make the case for the continued support of these facilities that make our work possible, we risk the government overlooking their impact.
